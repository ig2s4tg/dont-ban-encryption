Encryption has been in the news more than ever lately. The terrorist attack in San Bernardino brought the notion of government-controlled backdoors to national headlines. Some officials claim that encryption prevented investigators from doing their job and warn that it could provide a secure channel for terrorists to communicate in the future. And to many people, encryption may sound like technological black magic that hackers and terrorists can use to cover their tracks. The purpose of this page is to provide some insight into what encryption is and why trying to control its use is a bad idea.

The Basics

In a computer, all data is stored in the form of numbers. Because computers use electrical signals store and process information, it’s easiest to represent numbers in binary: an electrical current means 1, no current means 0. Computer scientists have come up with standards for representing things that aren’t as straightforward as integers; for instance, characters are often directly mapped to numbers (“A”=65, “B”=66… see this table) and larger things like pictures and videos are stored in agreed-upon formats like png or mp4.

Transforming Words

Let’s say you’re in grade school and you want to pass a note to your friend, Alice, on the other side of the room. You can’t get up and walk across the room, because then the teacher would see you and you’d get in trouble. You can however, hand the note to the person next to you and say “pass this to Alice.” Of course, you're trusting everyone between you and Alice not to open the note, and there's a pretty good chance someone will. So you and Alice come up with a way to encode your messages: shift the letters through the alphabet by a number you’ve agreed upon before class. So, if your secret number is 4,

HELLO ALICE!

becomes

MJQQT FQNHJ!

Now you can pass notes with a little more security. You’ve just implemented a Caesar cipher, one of the simplest and earliest forms of encryption. (It got it’s name because Julius Caesar used it!)

Transforming Numbers

What if you were to translate the letters to their binary representation beforehand? You could do much more than just add or subtract. If you represent the values in their binary form, you could use boolean operators to transform them. Boolean operators denote functions that affect boolean data kind of like addition and subtraction affect base-ten integers. For example, the functions AND and OR take two inputs and produce a result. AND only results in a 1 if both of the inputs are 1’s, but OR will be 1 if either of them are. XOR (eXclusive OR), will be 1 if either of the inputs are 1, but 0 if they both are. What makes XOR special (and useful) is that if you XOR something, you can XOR it again to get your original value back.

So if you wanted to encrypt a message to send to Alice, you could write your number in binary and, using a secret word (also converted to binary) as the second input, you could send a much more secure message. This would obviously be a pain to do by hand, but that’s where computers come in.

So That’s It?

Not quite. Industry-standard encryption gets pretty (read: extremely) complex. It makes use of really big prime numbers because of how long it takes to find prime factors. One such method, RSA, uses two numbers to make two keys, one that “locks” (transforms the data into gibberish) and one that “unlocks” (reverse the transformation, revealing the decrypted data). One key is your public key, which you can freely distribute to anyone who wants to send you something. Someone can use your public key to encrypt their message, send it to you, and know that only you can decrypt it because only you know the corresponding private key. There are many more methods or encryption that serve many more purposes, like the one encrypting your connection to this site.

Why It Matters

“Former NSA Director Michael Hayden said America is more secure and safer with unbreakable end to end encryption. I can promise you ... one thing: If I am standing shoulder to shoulder with the director of the National Security Agency on something, there’s a damn good reason for that. "
-Edward Snowden

Our society is growing increasingly dependent on the internet. Shopping, banking, private communication- even some cars and refrigerators rely on the web. Without a secure means of transaction, your interactions with your bank or business partner could be up for grabs. As for backdoors, introducing a backdoor into a system inherently flaws it; there’s mathematically no way to give only the good guys access.

A Few More Points

The citizens of the United States aren’t the only ones concerned about the position of the U.S. Government — The European Parliament released a statement to the U.S. last year which acknowledged that “[they] have different ideas on privacy and platforms, net neutrality and encryption, Bitcoin, zero-days or copyright,” and stresses the importance of “close cooperation between the EU and the US.”

Many of the most notable modern cryptologists authored a paper detailing their exploration of the effects that “imposing extraordinary access mandates” could have on the security of the internet. They found that “the damage that could be caused by law enforcement exceptional access requirements would be even greater today that it would have been 20 years ago.” (In 1993, the NSA introduced the Clipper chip, a chipset that served as an encryption device with a built in backdoor. It was defunct and abandoned by 1996.) They go on to state that even beyond technical vulnerabilities, “the prospect of globally deployed exceptional access systems raises difficult problems about how such an environment would be governed and how to ensure that such systems would respect human rights and the rule of law.”
